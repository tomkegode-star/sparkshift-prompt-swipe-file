Most Valuable

The One-Prompt Setup

This is the single most valuable thing in this entire guide. A CLAUDE.md file is a set of permanent instructions that Claude reads every time it starts a new conversation. Think of it as a briefing document. You write it once, and Claude remembers it forever.

Below is a real CLAUDE.md, annotated so you can see what each section does and adapt it for your own work.

A Real CLAUDE.md (Annotated)

# Claude Code Global Instructions

## Windows Environment
- File tools: Use backslashes (C:\Users\Charles\...)
- Expand ~ to C:\Users\Charles
- Shell: PowerShell 7 (pwsh.exe)Click to copy
## Terminal Output
Working: Blockquotes (>), Tables, Lists, Horizontal rules
Not working: Headers, bold, italic, inline codeClick to copy
## Browser Tools
- Default: Use Playwright for web browsing
- Each project can override this in its own CLAUDE.mdClick to copy

Interview me about what I need. Ask me as many questions as you can to understand my situation, my goals, what I've tried before, and what "done" looks like for me. Don't start building until you've asked at least 10 questions.Click to copy

Quick Wins

Magic Phrases

These aren't full prompts. They're short sentences, sometimes just a few words, that consistently make Claude work better. I use these more than anything else.

Think of these as steering-wheel adjustments. Small turns, big difference.

"Use your best judgment."

When you're stuck or don't know what to decide. Claude asks "do you want me to use approach A or approach B?" and you don't know the difference. Just say this. Let it decide.

"Interview me about what I need."

When you don't know where to start. When you've got a fuzzy idea in your head but can't articulate it. Let Claude ask the questions.

"No, I'm pretty sure you can."

Works brilliantly for websites, marketing funnels, business processes, contracts, content strategies. Anything where you know the destination but not the route.

"No, you do it."

When Claude tells you it can't do something. Claude defaults to saying "no" as a safety measure, not because it's actually incapable. Push back.

"Don't ask. Just do it."

When Claude keeps checking in with you. Some tasks don't need a committee. Give permission to act autonomously.

"Pretend I'm not technical."

The universal translator. Add this to any request and Claude shifts from jargon to clarity.

"What would you do?"

When you want an opinion, not just options. Claude can be surprisingly good at making judgment calls when you give it permission.

"Be honest. Is this good?"

Claude defaults to being encouraging. Sometimes you need the truth. This phrase gives it permission to be critical.

Workflows

Prompt Chains

Single prompts are great. But the real momentum happens when you chain them. The output of one becomes the input of the next. Each step builds on the last.

Chain 1: Client Onboarding

Interview me about my new client [name]. Ask me everything you'd need to know to scope a project for them.Click to copy

→ What you get: Structured notes from the interview. All the important details pulled out of your head and organised.

Now turn that into a scope document. Include outcomes, timeline, and what's out of scope.Click to copy

→ What you get: A professional scope doc you'd normally spend an hour writing.

Write a proposal email to [client name] based on that scope. Keep it warm but professional.Click to copy

→ What you get: A ready-to-send proposal email that sounds like you, not a robot.

Chain 2: Content Pipeline

Interview me about [topic I know well]. Ask me questions until you've got enough for a solid blog post.Click to copy
Now write the blog post based on that conversation. Use my voice, my examples, my opinions.Click to copy
Now create 5 social media posts that promote this article. Different angles, different hooks.Click to copy

Chain 3: Strategy Sprint

Interview me about my business. Ask me about my customers, revenue, challenges, and goals.Click to copy
Based on that, identify the 3 highest-impact opportunities I should focus on. For each one, give me a 30-day action plan.Click to copy
Now build me a simple tracking dashboard where I can mark progress on each action item.Click to copy

P1–P8

Prompting Principles

The rules behind the prompts. Understanding these will make every prompt you write, including your own, work better.

P1: Start with the end

Tell Claude what "done" looks like before you tell it what to do. "I want a one-page summary" beats "summarise this" every time.

P2: Show, don't describe

A screenshot beats a paragraph. An example beats an explanation. Paste in what you like. Drag in what you want. Claude works from examples better than instructions.

P3: One thing at a time

Don't ask Claude to build a website, write copy, and design a logo in the same breath. Stack small wins. Each prompt builds on the last.

P4: Give context, not instructions

"I run a coaching business with 12 clients" gives Claude more to work with than "write something professional." Context creates quality.

P5: Say what you don't want

Blacklists work. "Don't use jargon." "No bullet points." "Avoid the word 'delve.'" Removing bad habits is faster than describing good ones.

P6: Let Claude ask questions

"Interview me" is the most underrated prompt. Let Claude do the thinking about what it needs to know. You'd be surprised how good its questions are.

P7: Build in public with Claude

Don't try to get it perfect in one go. Ship something rough, then iterate. "Make this better" is a perfectly valid prompt.

P8: Save what works

When a prompt works well, save it. Put it in your CLAUDE.md. Build a library. Your future self will thank you.
